Chapter 1 · The catalogue moment

The buyer opens the catalogue — and sees everything the supplier must fulfil

A buyer in Hamburg opens a paint-maker’s product catalogue. Before a single order is placed, the catalogue shows not just the product — but every clearance, certificate and cover this supplier must hold to sell this product into this market. Nothing is buried in a profile or promised by email. It is in front of the buyer’s eyes, each with its trust rung.

Chapter 2 · Facilitating the supplier

For the buyer to see “fulfilled”, the supplier is facilitated to get there

The reason a firm stays out of export is rarely the product — it is that amber REACH line, and the months of ECHA paperwork behind it. So the rail doesn’t just show the gap; it facilitates the supplier to close it: step-by-step guidance, or a governing partner who holds the ECHA relationship and files on the supplier’s behalf. The certificate that comes back is a real artifact — and it lands on the rail.

Chapter 3 · Across the engine

The real evidence moves across the rail — and each side keeps its own copy

When the order is placed, the documents don’t sit in an inbox or a shared drive. Each is a chit that travels the rail between seller and buyer, replicated per copy: the seller holds theirs, the buyer holds an independent one, and neither can quietly alter the other’s. This is the engine carrying real template evidence.

Chapter 4 · The commercial spine

Beneath the chemical certificates, the same commercial risks — in every industry

Strip away the SDS and the REACH file and you find the part that never changes. The buyer and seller face the same four risks whatever they trade, named in the language the discipline already uses — FRM. This is why the spine is invariant: it is risk, not chemistry.

FRM · credit
Will I be paid?
Letter of Credit · guarantees · export-credit cover.
FRM · operational
Lost in transit?
Marine cargo & liability insurance — routed by the Incoterm.
FRM · market
Rate moves?
FX forwards & options — the hedge, not the cover.
FRM · liquidity
Cash-flow gap?
Pre/post-shipment finance · factoring.
Chapter 5 · Settlement & the self-proving close

Documents meet payment, the deal settles, and the trade proves itself

At settlement the LC releases against a complying presentation — the very SDS, CoA, bill of lading and insurance certificate that travelled the rail. The goods clear because every clearance the buyer saw at the catalogue moment turned out to be real. And when it closes, the deal writes itself into both parties’ track records — a reference the next buyer can trust, because both sides hold the matching copy.

Everything the buyer was promised at the catalogue was evidence on the rail — so at settlement there is nothing left to argue, only documents to match.
